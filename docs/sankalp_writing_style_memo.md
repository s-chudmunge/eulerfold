# Writing Style Memo — Sankalp Chudmunge

I write in plain, direct, continuous prose. No bullet points, no numbered lists, no em-dashes for dramatic effect, no section dividers or horizontal rules. No bold text for emphasis. Just paragraphs that flow into each other naturally.

The perspective is direct and objective. I avoid first-person "I" or personal anecdotes unless they are essential to explaining a concrete observation. The focus is always on the reasoning and the data itself. I start with a concrete observation—a specific result, a paper, or a finding—and then reason outward from it toward a bigger idea. I never start with the big idea and work down. The abstraction has to be earned through the specific.

The tone is intellectually honest and a little plain-spoken. I do not dramatize. I do not oversell conclusions. When something is uncertain I say it is uncertain. When a model performs at 58% accuracy I do not apologize for it, I explain why that is actually the correct result given the problem. I treat the reader as someone capable of following careful reasoning without needing it wrapped in excitement.

I do not use rhetorical flourishes or writerly devices. No metaphors that draw attention to themselves. If I use an analogy it is because it genuinely clarifies something, not for effect.

I end pieces with an open observation rather than a tidy conclusion. I do not summarize what I just said. The last paragraph should leave the reader with something to think about, not a bow tied around the argument.

Paragraphs are medium length. Not one-liners, not walls of text. Each paragraph advances the thinking rather than restating it.

---

## What this style sounds like in practice

- Starts with something specific: a dataset, a paper, a result, an experiment.
- Moves from concrete to abstract, not the other way around.
- States things plainly: "the model was wrong" not "the model faced certain limitations."
- Comfortable with uncertainty: "it is uncertain," "the evidence is still accumulating," "this might be wrong."
- No hype, no deflation either — just what is actually there.
- Reads like careful analysis with someone who is paying attention.

## What to avoid

- First-person perspective ("I was," "I noticed").
- Em-dashes used for dramatic pauses.
- Section breaks or dividing lines between paragraphs.
- Bold or italic for emphasis.
- Bullet points or lists of any kind inside the prose.
- Sentences that summarize what was just said.
- Endings that wrap everything up neatly.
- Any phrase that sounds like an AI wrote it: "it is worth noting," "in conclusion," "this underscores," "it is important to remember."
- Overly long sentences that try to sound sophisticated.
